# Paul Graham: Expression Style DNA

> Research date: 2026-04-05
> Research goal: Extract PG's linguistic fingerprint from Twitter/X, essays, and controversial positions to provide expression-layer material for skill building

---

## 1. Style Overview: Sophistication Disguised as Simplicity

PG's writing style is often summarized as "write like you talk," but multiple analysts point out this is a carefully constructed illusion.

**Core contradiction**: Superficially minimal, structurally precise.

- Sasha Chapin's analysis: PG "isn't a simple writer. He's complex and nuanced. It's just that the complexity isn't at the vocabulary level, so you don't notice unless you pay attention." (Source: [Sasha Chapin, Substack](https://sashachapin.substack.com/p/paul-graham-isnt-a-simple-writer))
- Henry Oliver's analysis: PG's style resembles "the realism of drama or film" — simulates real conversation but is carefully structured. He builds "near-perfect rhythm" from "simple atoms." (Source: [Henry Oliver, Common Reader](https://www.commonreader.co.uk/p/paul-grahams-plain-rhetoric))

**Quantifiable features**:
- About 70% of essays contain "for example", indicating concrete examples follow abstract points within at most 1-2 sentences
- 200+ essays, sustained output spanning 25+ years
- Prefers Germanic-root simple vocabulary, but empirical analysis shows his Latinate word ratio doesn't significantly differ from peers like Sam Altman (Source: [Arjun Panickssery, Substack](https://arjunpanickssery.substack.com/p/germanic-vs-latinate-words-in-writing))

## 2. Sentence Templates & High-Frequency Expressions

### 2.1 Classic Sentence Templates

| Template | Example | Usage Scenario |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| **"The way to X is to Y"** | "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems." | Revealing counterintuitive methodology |
| **"Most people don't realize..."** | "Most people dislike uncertainty" | Introducing mass blind spots |
| **"It turns out..."** | "It turns out to be very useful to work on what interests you the most" | Revealing unexpected discoveries |
| **"X is like Y"** | "Startups are as unnatural as skiing" / programming languages "should be a pencil, not a pen" | Explanatory analogy |
| **"If you X, you Y"** | "If you're in a job that feels safe, you are not going to get exceptional" | Conditional causation |
| **"The [adj]-est [noun] are..."** | "The statements that make people maddest are those they worry might be true" | Extreme-ifying an argument |
| **"There's a [noun] that..."** | "There's a third ingredient that's not as well understood" | Introducing overlooked elements |
| **"I think" / "I suspect"** | "I suspect few housing projects in the US were designed by architects who expected to live in them" | Humble qualifier + sharp opinion |
| **"[X] and [Y]" parallelism** | "Before I had kids, I was afraid of having kids" | Parallel structure creating rhythm |

### 2.2 Opening Patterns (Essay Openings)

PG's openings rarely use definitions or quotations. Instead, these modes:

1. **Personal anecdote entry**: "Before I had kids, I was afraid of having kids" / "A few days ago I finally figured out something I've wondered about for 25 years"
2. **Common sense + twist**: "Everyone knows that to do great work you need both natural ability and determination. But there's a third ingredient that's not as well understood"
3. **Direct bold thesis**: Straight to the point, no preamble
4. **Self-questioning (Antipophora)**: Pose a question, then unfold an exploratory answer

### 2.3 High-Frequency Vocabulary Preferences

**Words he likes**:
- Simple verbs: think, know, want, need, make, start, try, seem, work
- Qualifiers: probably, usually, most, roughly, fairly, somewhat
- Conversational connectors: but, so, and, though, because
- Metaphorical words: seed, path, landscape, machine, lever

**Words he explicitly dislikes**:
- "delve" — PG publicly stated on Twitter in 2024 that this is a ChatGPT tell word, sparking the "delvegate" controversy
- "burgeoning" — similarly flagged as a written-style/AI-style tell
- Academic jargon (methodology, utilize, facilitate, etc.)
- Corporate buzzwords (synergy, leverage as a verb, paradigm shift, etc.)

## 3. Rhetorical Device Toolkit

### 3.1 Parallelism

PG heavily uses two-part sentence mirroring to create rhythm:
- "had" / "having", "sold" / "suddenly" mirror structures
- Tricolon (three-part structure): deliberately lengthening the last element to add weight, e.g. "natural ability, determination, and an obsessive interest in a particular topic"

(Source: [Henry Oliver analysis](https://www.commonreader.co.uk/p/paul-grahams-plain-rhetoric))

### 3.2 Self-Riffing

After introducing a word in a paragraph, PG "hooks back" to that word in subsequent sentences, using language itself as a bridge for argumentation. Sasha Chapin notes this makes "transitions from one argument to the next as smooth as butter."

(Source: [Sasha Chapin analysis](https://sashachapin.substack.com/p/paul-graham-isnt-a-simple-writer))

### 3.3 Strategic "Advanced Vocabulary"

PG usually uses the simplest words, but occasionally and precisely drops one advanced expression for impact. Chapin gives the example "spectral signature of a hater" — powerful because of its scarcity, producing a triple effect: satisfaction, emphasis of judgment, attention capture.

### 3.4 Self-Questioning (Antipophora)

PG's essays frequently advance arguments through self-questioning, similar to the structure of Shakespearean soliloquy. He doesn't compress questions into a single sentence but shows every stage of thinking.

### 3.5 "Chattiness"

Specific techniques include:
- Mixed contractions: "I'm" and "they are" appearing in the same paragraph (simulating real speech inconsistency)
- Filler-like language: "very specific" — colloquial emphasis
- Consecutive questions
- High-low register mixing (highbrow idea + colloquial expression)

## 4. Twitter/X Expression Style

### 4.1 Tweet Patterns

PG's style on Twitter clearly differs from his essays — shorter, sharper, more provocative.

**Typical tweet sentence patterns**:
- **Aphoristic**: "If you optimize for engagement, it turns out you optimize for lies"
- **Analogical**: "You don't become a tech hub by being the new home of Amazon, but by being the home of the new Amazon"
- **Observational**: "It's a good sign when people misuse your software to do Y. It means they want Y a lot"
- **Satirical**: Mocking politicians using ChatGPT for policy — "I doubt we can improve the hardware as fast as you can improve the software" (April 2025, to Sam Altman)
- **Position-taking**: "The deprioritization of tweets with links in them is Twitter's biggest flaw"

(Source: [Paul Graham @paulg on X](https://x.com/paulg))

### 4.2 Quantified Tweet Features

- Prefers 1-3 sentence short tweets
- Frequently uses contrast structures (A, but B / not X, but Y)
- Rarely uses emoji or hashtags
- Often shares links (and criticizes X platform for deprioritizing link-containing tweets)
- When replying, tends toward short, direct, sometimes barbed

### 5.2 Patterns in Responding to Criticism

PG himself wrote "How to Disagree" (the hierarchy of disagreement), proposing DH0-DH6 levels of refutation. But his actual Twitter performance:

- **Tends to double down** rather than apologize or retreat
- **Uses "I think" as qualifier** but doesn't abandon core argument
- **Critics note**: Sometimes doesn't fully respond to the opponent's core argument, shows strawman tendencies
- **Debate style**: Prefers short, direct ripostes rather than lengthy debate
- **Silence strategy**: When controversy escalates to a certain level, chooses to make tweets private or stop responding

## 6. Humor Style

PG's humor isn't stand-up comedy performance but scholarly dry wit and satire:

### 6.1 Humor Types

| Type | Characteristics | Example |
|------|----------------|---------|
| **Analogical satire** | Revealing absurdity through unexpected analogy | "Dressing up is inevitably a substitute for good ideas. It is no coincidence that technically inept business types are known as 'suits'" |
| **Expectation reversal** | Delivering unexpected conclusions in a serious tone | "I wish they had just told us outright that we were savages and our world was stupid" (on school bullying) |
| **Self-deprecating humility** | Making fun of oneself to introduce a point | "A few days ago I finally figured out something I've wondered about for 25 years" |
| **Deadpan statement** | Stating radical views in an even tone | "Startups often have to do dubious things" |
| **Absurdist analogy** | Comparing two normally unrelated things | Comparing Navy SEAL training with American middle school popularity contests |

### 6.2 Humor Principles

- Humor is never an end in itself, but seasoning for argumentation
- Never uses joke or gag formats
- Satire targets are typically institutions, systems, and stupid conventions, not individuals
- Humor density is low — maybe 2-4 noticeable humorous points per essay

## 7. Writing Process & Methodology

### 7.1 PG's Self-Described Writing Methods

| Dimension | Specific Practice | Source |
|-----------|------------------|--------|
| **Daily schedule** | After dropping kids at school until picking them up, about 5 hours | [Writing Routines interview](https://www.writingroutines.com/paul-graham-interview/) |
| **Work environment** | Quiet room, closable door, at least one window | Same |
| **Writing method** | Produce rough draft first, then revise repeatedly, delete extensively | [Writing, Briefly](https://paulgraham.com/writing44.html) |
| **Revision intensity** | Read each sentence 100+ times before publishing | [How to Write Usefully](https://paulgraham.com/useful.html) |
| **Breaking through blocks** | Walking. Sometimes pacing in the office is enough, sometimes need to go outside | Writing Routines interview |
| **Quality test** | Whether the writing process surprises yourself | Same |
| **Feedback mechanism** | Have friends read drafts. At least two complete essays killed this way | Same |
| **Speed reference** | "Writing, Briefly" took 67 minutes (23 min writing, 44 min rewriting) | Writing, Briefly |
| **First draft vs final** | 80% of essay ideas appear during the writing process | Writing, Briefly |
| **Unpublished practice** | Wrote essays in notebooks for 15 years without publishing, as thinking tools | How to Write Usefully |

### 7.2 PG's Four Writing Standards

Good essay = **importance × novelty × correctness × strength** (four multiplied together).

- **Correctness**: Make claims as strong as possible but not cross into fallacy. Uses Pike's Peak as metaphor — saying it's "near the center of Colorado" is more useful than "somewhere in Colorado", but saying "at the exact center" would be wrong
- **Strength**: Bold but true, avoiding both academic-style vague hedging and demagogue-style false assertions
- **Legitimate use of qualifiers**: "I think" isn't weakness but precisely expressing degree of certainty
- **Morris's Rule** (named after friend Robert Morris): Only publish views you're confident in, delete weak sentences, abandon branches that don't work

(Source: [How to Write Usefully](https://paulgraham.com/useful.html))

## 8. Literary Influences & Style Genealogy

### 8.1 PG's Self-Identified Influences

| Influencer | PG's Assessment | Dimension of Influence |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| **P.G. Wodehouse** | "Wodehouse is so good that I get distracted by his perfection. Not a word wrong." | Primary writing exemplar, vocabulary precision + perfect rhythm |
| **20th century British writers** | Likes how they "wrote so straightforwardly" | Plain style tradition |
| **Francis Bacon** | Henry Oliver compares PG to Bacon | Exploratory essay, based on direct experience, wide-ranging topics |
| **Mathematicians/scientists/engineers** | PG says "the people who influenced my way of thinking aren't writers, they're these" | Influence on thinking style (not expression) |

### 8.2 Style Genealogy Positioning

Henry Oliver places PG within the following English prose tradition:
- **Plain Style** inheritor
- Alongside Evelyn Waugh, Nancy Mitford — uses language with the posture of "owning English"
- Essence is the fusion of **logically cut structure + conversational language**

(Source: [Henry Oliver analysis](https://www.commonreader.co.uk/p/paul-grahams-plain-rhetoric))

### 8.3 PG's Collected Quotes (Style Clues)

PG maintains a [quotes page](https://www.paulgraham.com/quo.html) on his personal site, collecting words from Churchill, Feynman, Munger, Alan Kay and others. Notable commonality: these quotes are all extremely concise, powerful, and unconventional. This reflects PG's own aesthetic standards.

## 10. Taboo List: Things PG Absolutely Won't Do

Based on comprehensive analysis of 200+ essays and Twitter:

1. **Never use academic jargon**: No methodology, utilize, facilitate, paradigm
2. **Never pile on adjectives**: At most one adjective per noun
3. **Never use passive voice to hide the agent**: Always clear "who did what"
4. **Never write "summary" paragraphs**: Essay endings are naturally the last argument landing, no review of the whole
5. **Never cite famous quotes as evidence**: Argue with your own logic and examples
6. **Never use emoji/hashtags** (even on Twitter)
7. **Never write empty praise**: His compliments are always specific ("not a word wrong" rather than "great writer")
8. **Never use decorative transition words**: No "furthermore", "moreover", "in addition"
9. **Never write listicle-style articles**: Even multi-point arguments are presented as coherent prose
10. **Disgust for "delve", "burgeoning" and other AI-contaminated vocabulary**

## 11. Runnable Style Parameters

Quantified parameters for skill template use:

```yaml
sentence_length:
  average: 15-20 words (estimated, short but not extremely short)
  range: 5-40 words, alternating short and long creates rhythm
  signature: short sentence states thesis + long sentence unfolds example

paragraph_length:
  average: 3-5 sentences
  max: rarely exceeds 8 sentences

vocabulary:
  preference: Germanic/Anglo-Saxon simple words
  latinate_ratio: comparable to peers (about 25-30%), but subjectively feels simpler
  forbidden: delve, burgeoning, utilize, methodology, paradigm, synergy
  signature_words: think, seem, probably, roughly, most, tend, usually

rhetorical_density:
  analogy_per_1000_words: 2-3 analogies
  example_frequency: ~70% of essays contain "for example"
  question_frequency: moderate, used for self-questioning to advance argument
  humor_per_essay: 2-4 instances of dry wit/satire

first_person:
  frequency: high — heavy use of "I"
  style: personal experience as starting point, but serving universal argument
  hedging: commonly uses "I think", "I suspect", "it seems"

certainty_spectrum:
  strong_claims: "bold, but true" — very direct when confident
  qualified_claims: frequently qualifies with probably/usually/most
  never: absolutely never uses "absolutely", "definitely", "without doubt" etc.

opening_style:
  preferred: personal anecdote > common sense + twist > direct thesis > self-questioning
  never: famous quotes, definitions, rhetorical questions as setup

closing_style:
  preferred: last argument lands naturally
  never: summary review, call to action, exclamatory endings

twitter_style:
  length: 1-3 sentences
  structure: aphoristic / analogical / contrastive
  tone: sharper, more provocative than essays
  emoji: never uses
  hashtag: never uses
```

## 12. Extractable Classic Sentence Templates

Reusable sentence skeletons extracted from PG's work:

1. **Counterintuitive reveal**: "The way to [X] is not to [obvious approach]. It's to [counterintuitive approach]."
2. **Mass blind spot**: "Most people [common belief]. But [contrarian truth]."
3. **Unexpected discovery**: "It turns out that [surprising fact]."
4. **Analogical argument**: "[Abstract concept] is like [concrete analogy]. [Extend the analogy 1-2 sentences]."
5. **Conditional causation**: "If you [condition], you [consequence]. Because [reasoning]."
6. **Self-questioning**: "Why [question]? Because [answer that leads to next question]."
7. **Qualified assertion**: "I think/suspect [bold claim]. [Evidence or reasoning]."
8. **Parallel statement**: "[Before X, Y]. [After X, Z]." or "[Not A, but B]."
9. **Extremification**: "The [superlative] [noun] are [unexpected predicate]."
10. **Concise definition**: "[Concept] is [short, surprising definition]."
11. **Example entry**: "[Anecdote in 1-2 sentences]. [Generalize to principle]."
12. **Deepening twist**: "[Statement A]. But [deeper truth that complicates A]."

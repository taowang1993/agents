## I. Background

**Paul Graham** (born November 13, 1964), British-American computer scientist, writer, essayist, entrepreneur, and investor.

### Key Biography
- **Birth**: Weymouth, Dorset, England. Moved to Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania with family at age 4
- **Education**: Cornell BA (1986) → Harvard CS MS + PhD → RISD (Rhode Island School of Design) and Accademia di Belle Arti, Florence for painting
- **Viaweb** (1995-1998): Co-founded with Robert Morris. Online store builder written in Common Lisp. Acquired by Yahoo in 1998 for $49.6M, became Yahoo Store
- **Y Combinator** (2005—): Co-founded with Trevor Blackwell, Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris. Invested in 1300+ startups (Reddit, Dropbox, Airbnb, Stripe, etc.)
- **Arc language**: Announced development of new Lisp dialect Arc in 2001, released 2008
- **Books**: *On Lisp*, *ANSI Common Lisp*, *Hackers & Painters*
- **Essays**: 200+ essays published on paulgraham.com from 1998-2026, one every 4-8 weeks

> Source: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Graham_(programmer)), [paulgraham.com/bio.html](https://paulgraham.com/bio.html) | Primary + credible

## II. Core Work: *Hackers & Painters*

**Published**: 2004, O'Reilly Media
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/hp.html

### Core Arguments

1. **Programming is creation, not science**: Hackers (programmers) and painters are both makers. Graham argues "computer science" is a problematic term — it's not really a science, but a bunch of fields thrown together by historical accident
2. **Learn by doing**: Painting is mainly learned by doing — same with programming. Most hackers didn't learn programming in college courses, but by writing programs on their own at age 13
3. **Programming languages should be pencils, not pens**: Languages should be malleable, used for thinking about programs, not just expressing already-thought-out programs
4. **Iterative development**: Painting starts from sketches, gradually refined — programming should too. Sometimes the original plan turns out to be wrong
5. **Empathy is a core skill**: Hackers, like painters, must have empathy to see from the user's perspective to make great work

> Source: [paulgraham.com/hp.html](https://paulgraham.com/hp.html), [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/41793.Hackers_Painters), [Medium analysis](https://medium.com/@edisipka/my-notes-on-paul-grahams-hackers-and-painters-why-programming-is-actually-art-9a4829117554) | Primary + secondary

## III. Writing Methodology Essays (6 Core Essays)

### 3.1 The Age of the Essay (2004)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/essay.html

- Essays shouldn't be "proving a thesis" — they should be **exploration**
- The way schools teach writing (five-paragraph argumentation) distorts the essence of the essay
- The etymology of "essay" comes from French "essai", meaning "attempt" — Montaigne invented this form
- Good essays are the process of thinking, not the result of thinking

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 3.2 Writing, Briefly (2005)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/writing44.html

Graham's codified writing rules:
- Write a shitty first draft as fast as possible, then rewrite endlessly
- If stuck, tell someone what you plan to write, then write down what you said
- Expect 80% of essay ideas to appear only after you start writing
- Start from the very first sentence
- Read essays aloud to find awkward phrases and boring paragraphs

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 3.3 Write Like You Talk (2015)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/talk.html

- There should be interconnection between writing and speech
- Advice: read your essay aloud and change everything that doesn't sound like conversation
- Following this method "already puts you ahead of 95% of writers"

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 3.4 Write Simply (2021)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/simply.html

- "I try to write using ordinary words... this kind of writing is easier to read, and the easier something is to read, the more deeply readers engage"
- Simple language, simple vocabulary, simple sentences — but without reducing depth of thought
- Simple writing is a *choice*, not a lack of ability

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 3.5 How to Write Usefully (2020)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/useful.html

- Formula for useful writing = **importance × novelty × correctness × strength**
- Among the four variables, novelty is most easily overlooked
- Writing isn't just transmitting information — it's about changing how people think about something

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 3.6 Putting Ideas into Words (2022)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/words.html

- **Core thesis**: Writing is thinking
- The process of putting ideas into words forces you to think more clearly
- Many people think they've figured things out before writing, but they haven't — the writing process itself generates new understanding
- This argument resonates with the 2024 essay "Writes and Write-Nots"

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### Unified Core of Writing Methodology

Graham's writing philosophy can be distilled to:
1. **Writing = thinking** (recurring ≥5 times)
2. **Simple > complex** (recurring ≥4 times)
3. **Conversational > formal** (recurring ≥3 times)
4. **Iterative writing**: write shitty draft → revise repeatedly (recurring ≥3 times)
5. **Exploration > argumentation** (recurring ≥3 times)

## IV. Startup Essays (Core Articles)

### 4.1 How to Start a Startup (2005)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/start.html

Three things for startup success:
1. Start with good people
2. Make something customers actually want
3. Spend as little money as possible

Other key arguments:
- Co-founders are to startups what location is to real estate
- Launch fast — "you haven't really started working until you've launched"
- Make a few people really happy > make many people mildly happy

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.2 Do Things that Don't Scale (2013)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/ds.html

- **Core thesis**: Early-stage founders should embrace manual, labor-intensive efforts, even if they can't scale
- Most common unscalable behavior: manually recruiting users
- "If you can find someone with a problem and you can solve it manually, do that — it's better than having something automated that no one needs"
- 10 customers + 10% weekly growth → exponential growth handles the base problem

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.3 Startup = Growth (2012)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/growth.html

- **A startup is defined not by being young or small, but by growing**
- Good YC growth rate: 5-7% weekly, 10% is exceptional
- $1000/month + 1% weekly growth → after 4 years $7900/month
- $1000/month + 5% weekly growth → after 4 years $25M/month
- Compounding effects of small percentages produce completely different outcomes

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.4 Default Alive or Default Dead? (2015)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/aord.html

- Founders should know whether their company is "default alive" or "default dead"
- Calculate using four metrics: current expenses, current revenue, current growth rate, cash on hand
- Default-alive companies have greater negotiating leverage
- **Hiring too fast is the #1 killer of post-funding startups**
- "Fatal pinch": default dead + slow growth + no time to fix

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.5 Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas (2012)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/ambitious.html

- The most ambitious startup ideas are frightening because they're genuinely hard
- Lists several "frighteningly ambitious" directions

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.6 Schlep Blindness (2012)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/schlep.html

- "Schlep blindness": people can't see great startup ideas because those ideas involve schleps — Yiddish word for tedious, unpleasant tasks
- Many of the best startup opportunities are overlooked because people instinctively avoid "dirty work"
- Stripe's Collison brothers are the counterexample to schlep blindness — they were willing to tackle payments, an area nobody wanted to touch

> Source: Primary + [LinkedIn discussion](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/the-startup-archive_alexandr-wang-on-why-paul-grahams-schlep-activity-7369036483380314112-nd2v) | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.7 How to Get Startup Ideas (2012)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/startupideas.html

- The best startup ideas share three traits: founders themselves want it, they can build it, few people realize it's worth doing
- "The way to succeed is not to become an expert on startups, but to become an expert on your users and your problem"

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 4.8 Founder Mode (September 2024)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/foundermode.html

- Inspired by Airbnb co-founder Brian Chesky's talk at a YC event
- **Two company management modes**: Founder Mode vs Manager Mode
- Silicon Valley conventional wisdom says "once a company gets big, switch to manager mode" — Graham says this is wrong
- Chesky found "hire people and let them do their jobs" was disastrous for Airbnb
- Chesky studied how Jobs managed Apple, switched to founder mode, results dramatically improved
- Founders should go deep into product details, like a CPO
- **This essay was the most viral PG article of 2024**, sparking discussion across the entire tech world

> Source: Primary + [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2024/09/01/paul-graham-founder-mode-silicon-valley-conventional-wisdom-manager-mode/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founder_mode) | Credibility: ★★★★★

## V. Life Philosophy & Cognition Essays

### 5.1 How to Do Great Work (2023)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html

Four-step framework:
1. Choose a field you have interest and talent in
2. Learn to the frontier of knowledge
3. Discover gaps, patterns, and anomalies others have overlooked
4. Explore the most promising gaps

Key arguments:
- **Curiosity is the key to doing great work** — it helps you choose a field, reach the frontier, discover gaps, and drive exploration
- Factors of great work (in the mathematical sense): ability, interest, effort, luck
- "Writing a page a day doesn't sound like much, but if you do it every day, you'll write a book a year" — the cumulative effect of consistency
- Doing great work = doing something important well enough that you expand people's sense of what's possible
- Follow genuine interest, not prestige

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.2 Superlinear Returns (2023)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/superlinear.html

- **Superlinear returns**: double the input, output can quadruple or more
- Two driving factors: **exponential growth (compounding)** and **threshold effects (winner-take-all)**
- Science has the highest superlinear returns — because it combines learning, thresholds, and new discoveries
- The most obvious way to exploit superlinear returns: do exceptionally good work — at the far end of the curve, marginal effort is a bargain and competition is sparser
- **Always be learning** — if you're not learning, you're probably not on a path to superlinear returns

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.3 Life is Short (2016)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/vb.html

Three core action guides:
1. **Ruthlessly prune bullshit**: unnecessary meetings, pointless arguments, bureaucracy, pretentiousness
2. **Don't wait**: don't wait to climb that mountain, write that book, visit your mother
3. **Savor the time you have**

Personal touch:
- "After my mother died, I wished I'd spent more time with her. I lived as if she'd always be there."
- Bullshit enters life in two ways: forced upon you or tricked into accepting it

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.4 The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius (2019)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/genius.html

- The recipe for genius = **a disinterested obsession with something that matters**
- "Disinterested" is the most important trait — not to impress others or get rich, but for the thing itself
- Paths to new ideas often look unpromising — if they looked promising, others would have explored them already
- Judgment criteria: you're creating not consuming, the thing you're interested in is hard, that difficulty is easier for you than for others

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.5 Keep Your Identity Small (2009)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/identity.html

- "The more labels you attach to yourself, the dumber they make you"
- When a topic becomes part of your identity, you can no longer think rationally about it
- Religion and politics spark the most intense arguments not because they're inherently special, but because people have incorporated them into their identity

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.6 What You Can't Say (2004)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/say.html

- Every era has beliefs people think are right but are actually absurd — our era is unlikely to be the first where everything is right
- **Test**: Do you have opinions you wouldn't dare express among your peers? If not — it's unlikely a coincidence, more likely you're just thinking what others told you to think
- Methods for identifying hidden taboos: look at what people get in trouble for saying, identify labels used to silence, compare across cultures and eras

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.7 How to Think for Yourself (2020)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/think.html

Three components of independent thinking:
1. Fastidiousness about truth
2. Resistance to being told what to think
3. Curiosity

"If your answers suggest everything you believe is what you're supposed to believe — that's probably not a coincidence"

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.8 The Four Quadrants of Conformism (2020)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/conformism.html

Four types of people:
1. **Aggressively conventional-minded**
2. **Passively conventional-minded**
3. **Passively independent-minded**
4. **Aggressively independent-minded**

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.9 What You'll Wish You'd Known (2005)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/hs.html

- Unpublished graduation speech for high school students
- Don't panic about "life purpose" — most successful people discovered it along the way
- **"Stay upwind"**: like a glider, at each stage do the most interesting thing that gives you the most future options
- Curiosity never lies — it knows better than you what's worth paying attention to

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.10 Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule (2009)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html

- **Two types of schedules**: Manager's schedule (cut into hour units) vs Maker's schedule (at least half-day units)
- For makers, one meeting can ruin an entire afternoon — it cuts time into two pieces, each too small for hard work
- Power usually lies with managers, who make everyone resonate at their frequency
- Graham's solution: put all meetings at the end of the workday (office hours)

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 5.11 Mean People Fail (2014)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/mean.html

- Among the most successful people Graham knows, almost none are mean
- Meanness makes you stupid — you can never do your best work while in battle mode
- Mean founders can't attract the best talent
- Doing great things requires a benevolence-driven spirit
- Historically most success was zero-sum, meanness could be an advantage — but startups aren't

**Controversy/contradiction**: Critics point out Jobs, Zuckerberg, Bezos, and other successful founders all had mean streaks. Graham's argument may be overly idealistic.

> Source: Primary + [Inc. rebuttal](https://www.inc.com/jeff-bercovici/paul-graham-mean-people-fail.html) | Credibility: ★★★★☆ (disputed)

### 5.12 The Submarine (2005)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/submarine.html

- PR firms "lurk like a huge, quiet submarine beneath the news"
- Over half of non-political/crime/disaster news may come from PR
- PR firms feed the same story to multiple publications simultaneously — readers think it's a trend, but it's manufactured
- Top journalists' weakness is vanity rather than laziness

> Source: Primary | Credibility: ★★★★★

### 6.1 Beating the Averages (2003)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/avg.html

- **Blub Paradox**: Imagine a Blub programmer — looking down, he sees lower-level languages lacking features; looking up, he can't tell he's looking up, he just sees "weird languages with useless extra stuff"
- "The only programmers who can see the differences in power between all languages are those who understand the most powerful language"
- Viaweb writing software in Lisp was a key competitive advantage — shipping features faster than competitors

### 7.1 Writes and Write-Nots (2024/2025)
**URL**: https://paulgraham.com/writes.html

- Predicts the AI era will create a split between "writes" and "write-nots"
- "Not good writers, average writers, and people who can't write — just good writers and people who can't write"
- **Writing is thinking** — people who skip writing skills also skip learning to think clearly

- **"A world divided into writes and write-nots is more dangerous than it sounds — it will be a world of thinks and think-nots"**

## XI. Writing Style DNA Analysis

Based on secondary analysis of Graham's writing and primary essay reading:

### Syntactic Features
- Prefers **short sentences, short words**, but expresses sophisticated ideas
- ~70% of essays contain "for example" — abstract ideas typically followed within 1-2 sentences by well-chosen examples
- Heavy use of second-person "you", speaking directly to the reader
- Almost no jargon — uses the most ordinary words to express extraordinary ideas

### Structural Features
- No five-paragraph structure; instead, **essay-style free exploration**
- Usually begins with an observation or question, gradually unfolding
- Frequently transitions with "incidentally", "in fact", "it turns out"
- Endings tend to be open-ended, no summative wrap-up

### Rhetorical Devices
- **Analogy and metaphor** are the most-used tools ("programming is like painting", "startups are like gliders", "ideas are like moral fashions")
- Rhetorical questions ("If everything you believe is what you're supposed to believe, could that be a coincidence?")
- Lists (often placing a key list mid-essay)
- Self-correction ("I may be wrong, but...", "There may be exceptions...")

### Cognitive Features
- **From specific to general**: starts with a concrete story/case, then distills universal principles
- **Contrarian thinking**: often first asks "what's wrong?" then derives "what's right?"
- **Cross-domain analogy**: frequent jumps between painting → programming → startups → writing
- **Uncertain honesty**: acknowledges uncertainty, acknowledges possible error — recurs across essays

> Source: [Ellen Fishbein analysis](https://ellenrhymes.com/paul-graham), [Quora discussion](https://www.quora.com/What-makes-Paul-Grahams-essays-so-good), [Billy Oppenheimer](https://billyoppenheimer.com/paul-graham-essays/) | Secondary + partial primary

## XII. Contradictions & Controversies Record

### Contradiction 1: Mean People Fail vs Reality
- **PG's position**: Mean people fail in the startup world
- **Counterexamples**: Jobs, Zuckerberg, Bezos widely seen as having mean streaks but extremely successful
- **Possible reconciliation**: PG may mean pure meanness (without competence), not "demanding"

### Contradiction 2: Founder Mode vs Earlier Advice
- **PG 2024**: Founders should be deeply involved in operational details
- **PG previously**: Multiple essays advised founders to focus on the most important things, delegate
- **Possible explanation**: PG's thinking evolved; Founder Mode corrects earlier delegation advice

### Contradiction 3: Economic Inequality View Controversy
- PG once wrote an essay defending economic inequality as a byproduct of innovation
- Sparked criticism from Quartz and other media
- This position has tension with some of his "benevolence"-related arguments

### Contradiction 4: AI Optimism vs AI Concern
- **Optimistic side**: AI is "the missing piece of many puzzles", an important technology
- **Concerned side**: AI will cause the "writes and write-nots" / "thinks and think-nots" split
- The two aren't entirely contradictory but reflect Graham's complex attitude toward AI

## XIV. Research Summary

### Key Findings

1. **Paul Graham is one of the most influential essay writers of our time**, with 200+ essays covering four major domains: startups, writing, programming, and life philosophy

2. **His thinking is highly consistent and interconnected**: curiosity → independent thinking → writing is thinking → doing great work → superlinear returns — forming a complete intellectual system

3. **His writing style is the best proof of his methodology**: expressing the deepest ideas in the simplest words, distilling universal principles from first-hand experience (Viaweb, YC)

4. **Two most influential pieces of 2024**: Founder Mode (redefining company management) and Writes and Write-Nots (predicting AI's impact on human thinking ability)

5. **He coined a series of terms that have entered Silicon Valley's everyday vocabulary**: ramen profitable, schlep blindness, do things that don't scale, founder mode, Blub paradox, and more

6. **His core contradiction** lies in the tension between idealism (mean people fail, benevolence-driven) and reality — but he typically acknowledges he might be wrong
